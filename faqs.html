What to expect before you commit to anything

The discovery conversation costs nothing

Every engagement begins with a no-cost conversation. We look at what is already in place, where pressure may be showing up, and what a proportionate response could look like. No obligation beyond the conversation itself.

Nothing is introduced before a diagnostic

Before anything is recommended across any of the three service areas, a structured diagnostic is completed. What is delivered is built around your organisation — not applied from a template.

Fees are transparent and agreed in advance

If work beyond the discovery conversation is recommended, all fees are fully transparent and agreed before anything proceeds. In many cases, the savings identified offset the cost of the engagement.

Designed for organisations of 10 to 200 people

The programme scales to your size. We work with small volunteer-led charities, medium-sized not-for-profits, and organisations up to around 200 people. In every case, the approach is calibrated to fit.

You can engage with any combination of the three services. Many organisations begin with one area and expand the engagement over time as confidence and familiarity grows. The discovery conversation will help clarify which services are most immediately relevant to your situation.

Governance

Questions about governance structures, Investment Policy Statements, trustee responsibilities, and Charity Commission expectations.

An IPS is a formal document setting out your charity's investment objectives, risk appetite, ethical restrictions, and governance framework. If your charity has delegated discretionary investment management to an external manager, an IPS is a legal requirement under Charity Commission guidance. Many charities either do not have one, or hold a document that has not been reviewed in years.
At a minimum, annually. It should also be reviewed whenever circumstances change significantly — if your investment manager changes, if there is a substantial change in reserves, or if your charitable objects or ethical position evolves.
In some respects, smaller charities are more exposed — volunteer boards frequently lack the specialist time or expertise to manage governance obligations confidently. The Charity Commission actively encourages trustees to seek independent advice when they lack the time, knowledge, or inclination to act alone.
Yes. Adena Street operates entirely independently, with no commercial relationships with investment managers or product providers. Where specialist expertise is needed, it draws on the whole of the market — always in your interests.

Financial Guidance

Questions about investment performance, fees, asset returns, and how independent financial guidance works in practice.

Often, yes. Many trustees lack the benchmarking framework to assess performance accurately. A manager that appears to be performing well may be behind its peer group on a risk-adjusted basis, or may be charging fees that significantly reduce net returns. An independent review gives your board the evidence to confirm things are in good order — or to identify improvements.
An Ongoing Charges Figure is a fee embedded within the funds held in your investment portfolio. It is deducted directly from fund assets and frequently does not appear as a visible line on a statement. Over time, OCF charges can represent a material drag on net returns. Understanding the full cost of your investment arrangements is essential.
The ARC Charity Indices are sterling-denominated, risk-based benchmarks used to assess how a charity's investments have performed relative to peer charities. They provide a standardised comparison — if your manager is below the mean for their risk category over a sustained period, they are effectively in the bottom half of their peer group.
Yes. Cash management is one of the areas where charities most commonly leave value on the table. A structured review of your banking arrangements can identify meaningful improvements — particularly in a period where interest rates have shifted considerably.

Financial Wellbeing

Questions about staff financial wellbeing programmes, workshops, one-to-one guidance, and what the Aetas ITW approach looks like in practice.

An EAP typically provides reactive, crisis-focused support. It does not review your benefits structure, run financial resilience workshops, or provide one-to-one guidance from a regulated financial planner. Aetas ITW sits alongside an EAP, addressing proactive financial capability building before a crisis develops.
One-to-one sessions are available to all employees and provided at no charge to the individual. They are delivered confidentially by a regulated financial planner, by video call or in person. The employer does not receive any information about the content of individual sessions.
Workshop topics are designed around the specific profile and life stages of your workforce. Common areas include: day-to-day money management, understanding workplace benefits and pension entitlements, managing financial pressure, mid-career planning, and planning for later life.
Financial anxiety does not respect purpose. People who are genuinely motivated by their work are still vulnerable to practical financial pressures — and the salary gap between not-for-profit and private sector creates a specific tension that private sector employees do not face in the same way.
Aetas ITW is designed to be light on leadership time. The diagnostic conversation is typically around an hour. From there, we develop the programme, coordinate delivery, manage all employee communications, and provide ongoing oversight — without requiring your leadership team to manage providers or produce materials themselves.
The workshops and group sessions are educational and do not constitute regulated financial advice.
